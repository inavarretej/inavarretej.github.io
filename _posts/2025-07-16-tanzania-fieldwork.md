In July 2025, I had the chance to join Prof. Eric Calais for a two-week field campaign in northern Tanzania — one of those rare opportunities where the data you collect ends up directly feeding your PhD thesis.

## Why Tanzania?

The [Natron Basin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lake_Natron) sits in the eastern branch of the East African Rift, one of the most active continental rift systems on Earth. It is where the African plate is slowly tearing itself apart — a process driven by the interplay of tectonic faulting and magmatic intrusions that we are still trying to fully understand.

My research focuses on how deformation accumulates between major rifting events. To quantify this, we need precise measurements of how the ground is moving — and that is exactly what GNSS (Global Navigation Satellite System) stations provide.

## What We Measured

At each station, we collected high-precision horizontal (and vertical) velocity data. These velocities represent the integrated motion of the crust over the observation period — essentially, how fast and in which direction the ground is moving relative to stable Africa.

By comparing these geodetic velocities with seismic moment rates derived from earthquake catalogs, we can estimate what fraction of the ongoing deformation is released seismically and how much is accommodated through aseismic processes — things like slow fault creep, distributed deformation, or magma movement at depth.
